Bacterial Snack Attack Deactivates a Drug

Johnny Ha

A class of bacteria called Gammaproteobacteria has metabolic effects on anticancer drugs, which are drugs that help effectively treat cancerous diseases. Anticancer drugs can treat cancerous diseases by destroying the DNA of tumor cells, thereby killing the cancerous cells of the disease. Gammaproteobacteria belongs to the Enterobacteriaceae family and can be found in foods if food poisoning is encountered. For example, a well-known pathogen, E.coli belongs to this class of bacteria, thus, foods can be carrying this bacteria. The various effects were studied on mice, where E. coli was injected into their blood. The final results of the study shows that a gene present in some species within this class of bacteria, called cytidine deaminase gene, produces an enzyme that is able to metabolize certain chemotherapy drugs. The metabolized chemotherapy drugs turn into their inactive forms through a process called deamination, rendering them as ineffective cancer treatments. Bacteria normally does not enter the pancreatic cells of healthy individuals, therefore the resistance response is related to an immune function, leading to the theory that bacterial interactions with cells in the immune system influenced the effectiveness of cancer therapies. Therefore, cancer therapies are not 100% effective as advertised and known for. Overall, microbial metabolism is possibly a contributor to the deficient prognosis of cancer treatments.
